Wash

Building healthy hand washing habits through automated tracking

Overview

In class we were posed a question: How can we solve a daily problem people face? Since this was during the early days of COVID, before Shelter in Place, we aimed to reduce anxierty related to COVID.

We learned that people are less anxious when they feel they're taking steps to avoid getting sick. We identified a hand washing tracker as a way to make users feel in control of their health. We designed a watch app that seemlessly integrates this tracking into users’ existing routines.

Role

I was a UX Designer on a team of 3 design students. I focued on user research and synthesis, feature prioritization, user flow and journey mapping, and mocks and prototypes for the Apple Watch, and usability testing.

Timeline

3 week sprint, March 2020

The Goal

Decrease COVID-19 related anxiety in young urban professionals

Research Objectives

We began our sprint with user research to better understand how viral outbreaks impact mental health on young urban professionals

We needed answers to two questions:

  1. What makes people anxious about the contagious illnesses?
  2. What do people need to feel less anxious about contagious illnesses?

User Insights

We conducted 6 qualitative user interviews, on young urban professionals in the San Francisco Bay Area. We found three key insights

  1. People are anxious when they don’t have control over their health
  2. Taking protective actions make people feel in control
  3. The novelty of COVID-19 makes it difficult to know what actions to take

The Problem

Users need more information to be confident they’re protecting themselves from COVID

Ideation

From brainstorming we came up with three high level ideas:

  1. Live doctor chat to diagnose symptoms
  2. Personalized updates on COVID
  3. A checklist of preventative actions
By comparing what would be delightful to the user, with what’s feasible to build and good for society at scale we zeroed in on checklists of preventable actions users can take to protect themselves

Competitive Analysis

This kicked off a second round of ideation, for how to best design a hand washing checklist

We did a competitive analysis for inspiration, and the Apple Watch's Activity app led us to a solution that uses wearable sensors to passively track the user's hand washing behavior.

The Solution

Automatically track users’ hand washing behavior. Surface glanceable stats that remind users to wash their hands

Journey Map

We sketched out the likely way a day would play out using our watch app.

User Flow

We aimed for a subtle way to users to know that they’ve washed their hands a full 30 seconds. Users can rely on vibration instead of having to touch or even look at the screen.

Data Visualization Iteration

A critical part of the design, was creating a status meter that users could glance at on their watchface to learn whether they need to wash their hands. We iterated on a meter that fills when they app detects hand washing, and drains over time as the user’s protection from germs and viruses wears off

The Test

Do users understand what the app tracks, and what the visualizations and stats mean?

Improved Onboarding

Users had trouble comprehending core concepts, such as that the app automatically tracked hand washing, and that the height of the meter represented how protected their hands were.

We iterated on the onboarding flow to make these concepts clearer

The Result

When our test plan was performed again for the hi-fi iteration, all users comprehended the app’s tracking and stats

Learnings

Challenge assumptions

The most straightforward solution to our problem statement would have been a mobile app that would inform people about COVID. By not limiting ourselves to that during brainstorming we instead came up with a more novel solution that uses wearable technology to inform you when to wash your hands.

There can be too much transparency

At first we tried to explain exactly how long it would take for the hand wash meter to fully empty. This was too much information and confused users. With simpler stats users were able to more easily intuit a conceptual model for how it worked.

Opportunities

Environmental testing

Our short timeline, and shelter-in-place made it difficult to test how our design really fits into user's daily routine. An opportunity would be to prototype vibration during hand washing, or use journalling to see if a protoype does result in more frequent hand washingusability testing.

Snooze

We anticipate the above testing would show that sometimes hand washing reminders came at a bad time, and users would forget to wash their hands. Ability to snooze reminders would make them more effective
